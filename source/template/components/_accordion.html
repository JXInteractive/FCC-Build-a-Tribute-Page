Introduction

Carl Sagan (1934–1996) was an American astronomer, science communicator, author, media personality, and advocate for skepticism and critical thinking. During an illustrious academic career, he contributed in excess of 600 papers on topics related to astronomy, cosmology, astrophysics and astrobiology.

Sagan is credited on over 20 books—the sci-fi novel "Contact" (adapted into a film featuring Jodie Foster) his magnum opus. On television, he narrated and co-wrote "Cosmos: A Personal Journey", which—as of 2009—remained the most frequently watched PBS series.

Honours

Sagan won many awards throughout his life. The following are among his most notable:

  • Miller Institute's "Miller Research Fellowship" (1960–1962)
  • Klumpke-Roberts' "Award of the Astronomical Society of the Pacific" (1974)
  • National Aeronautics and Space Administration's "NASA Distinguished Public Service Medal" (1977)
  • The Ohio State University's "Annual Award for Television Excellence" (for PBS series 'Cosmos') (1981)
  • Hugo Award for 'Cosmos' (1981)
  • American Astronautical Society's "John F. Kennedy Astronautics Award" (1982)
  • Committee for the Scientific Investigation of Claims of the Paranormal's "Isaac Asimov Award" (1994)
  • SF Chronicle Award (for 'Contact') (1998)
